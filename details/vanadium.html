Vanadium (V)

Atomic Number 23 | Versatile Transition Metal

Overview

Vanadium is a chemical element with the symbol V and atomic number 23. It is a hard, silvery-grey metal that is primarily used to strengthen steel alloys. Vanadium's unique properties make it a key component in tools, construction, and energy storage technologies.

Discovered in 1801 by Andrés Manuel del Río, vanadium is found in minerals such as vanadinite and carnotite. It has a variety of oxidation states, contributing to its versatility in industrial applications.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 23
  • Atomic Mass: 50.9415 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Ar] 3d3 4s2
  • Group / Block: 5 (d-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 1910 °C
  • Boiling Point: 3407 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Vanadium is widely used in the production of steel alloys, where it improves strength and durability. It is also a critical component in vanadium redox flow batteries, which are used for large-scale energy storage. Additionally, vanadium compounds are utilized as catalysts in chemical processes and in ceramics production.

Historical Perspective

Vanadium was first discovered in 1801 by Andrés Manuel del Río, a Spanish-Mexican scientist, but it was later rediscovered by Nils Gabriel Sefström in 1831. The element was named after "Vanadis," the Norse goddess of beauty, reflecting its colorful compounds.

Today, vanadium is a vital material in high-strength steel production and emerging energy technologies, making it a key player in modern industry.
