Helium (He)

Atomic Number 2 | The Second Element

Overview

Helium is a chemical element with the symbol He and atomic number 2. It is a colorless, odorless, tasteless, non-toxic, inert, monatomic gas, the first in the noble gas group of the periodic table.

Helium is the second lightest element and the second most abundant in the observable universe. It was discovered through the analysis of the solar spectrum in 1868 by French astronomer Pierre Janssen and English astronomer Norman Lockyer, making it the first element detected outside Earth.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 2
  • Atomic Mass: 4.0026 u
  • Electron Configuration: 1s2
  • Group / Block: Noble Gas (p-block)
  • Standard State: Gas at 25 °C
  • Boiling Point: −268.928 °C (lowest among all elements)
  • Melting Point: −272.2 °C (under high pressures)
  • Radioactive: No

Helium has myriad uses, including cooling superconducting magnets in MRI machines, inflating balloons (both party balloons and weather balloons), and as a shield gas in welding. It is also critical in scientific research involving cryogenics.

Historical Perspective

Helium was first observed in the solar spectrum during a total solar eclipse in 1868. It was eventually isolated on Earth in 1895 by the British chemist Sir William Ramsay. The name "helium" originates from the Greek word for the Sun, "Helios," in reference to its cosmic discovery.

Today, helium is extracted from natural gas deposits, primarily in the United States. Concerns about helium scarcity have led to calls for more careful conservation and alternative uses to ensure its availability for critical applications.
