Phosphorus (P)

Atomic Number 15 | Essential Element for Life

Overview

Phosphorus is a chemical element with the symbol P and atomic number 15. It exists in several allotropes, the most common being white, red, and black phosphorus. Phosphorus is essential for life, playing a critical role in the structure of DNA, RNA, and ATP, as well as in cell membranes.

Discovered in 1669 by Hennig Brand, phosphorus was the first element to be discovered through a chemical process. It is primarily obtained from phosphate rock and is a key ingredient in fertilizers.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 15
  • Atomic Mass: 30.974 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Ne] 3s2 3p3
  • Group / Block: 15 (p-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 44.15 °C (white phosphorus)
  • Boiling Point: 280.5 °C (white phosphorus)
  • Radioactive: No

Phosphorus is primarily used in the production of fertilizers, which are essential for modern agriculture. Other applications include detergents, pesticides, and matches. Phosphorus compounds are also used in metal treatment, water treatment, and as flame retardants.

Historical Perspective

Phosphorus was discovered in 1669 by German alchemist Hennig Brand while he was experimenting with urine. Its name is derived from the Greek "phosphoros," meaning "light-bearer," due to its glowing properties when exposed to oxygen.

Since its discovery, phosphorus has been essential in various fields, including agriculture, medicine, and industry. Modern research continues to explore its role in biochemistry and renewable energy technologies.
