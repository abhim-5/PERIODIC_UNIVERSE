Copernicium (Cn)

Atomic Number 112 | Synthetic and Highly Radioactive Element

Overview

Copernicium is a chemical element with the symbol Cn and atomic number 112. It is a synthetic element that is not found naturally and is produced in particle accelerators. Copernicium is highly unstable, with its most stable isotope, Cn-285, having a half-life of about 29 seconds.

Copernicium was first synthesized in 1996 by a team of German scientists at the GSI Helmholtz Centre for Heavy Ion Research. The element is named after Nicolaus Copernicus, the astronomer who proposed the heliocentric model of the solar system.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 112
  • Atomic Mass: [285] u (most stable isotope)
  • Electron Configuration: [Rn] 5f14 6d10 7s2
  • Group / Block: 12 (d-block)
  • Standard State: Gas (predicted)
  • Melting Point: Unknown
  • Boiling Point: Unknown
  • Radioactive: Yes

Due to its short half-life and instability, copernicium has no practical applications. It is used exclusively in scientific research to study the properties of superheavy elements and to expand our understanding of the periodic table.

Historical Perspective

Copernicium was first synthesized in 1996 by a team of researchers led by Sigurd Hofmann. Its name honors Nicolaus Copernicus, whose revolutionary heliocentric model changed our understanding of the solar system.

Although it has no practical applications, the discovery of copernicium contributes to the scientific study of superheavy elements and their properties.
