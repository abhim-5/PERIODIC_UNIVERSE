Iron (Fe)

Atomic Number 26 | Essential Transition Metal

Overview

Iron is a chemical element with the symbol Fe (from Latin: ferrum) and atomic number 26. It is a silvery-gray metal known for its high strength and ability to form various alloys. Iron is the fourth most abundant element in the Earth's crust and an essential component of hemoglobin in living organisms.

Known since ancient times, iron has played a critical role in the development of tools, weapons, and structures, making it a cornerstone of human civilization.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 26
  • Atomic Mass: 55.845 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Ar] 3d6 4s2
  • Group / Block: 8 (d-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 1538 °C
  • Boiling Point: 2862 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Iron is predominantly used in the production of steel, an alloy of iron and carbon, which is a fundamental material in construction, transportation, and manufacturing. It is also utilized in magnets, electrical transformers, and as a dietary supplement.

Iron in Action

Iron is a versatile element with a wide range of applications, from structural materials to industrial machinery. Its magnetic properties and abundance in the Earth's crust make it a valuable resource for various industries.

Historical Perspective

Iron has been known and used for thousands of years, with its early usage dating back to 1200 BCE during the Iron Age. Its ability to be forged into tools and weapons revolutionized agriculture and warfare.

Today, iron remains a critical material in modern industry, underscoring its versatility and importance in various applications.
