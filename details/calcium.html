Calcium (Ca)

Atomic Number 20 | Essential Alkaline Earth Metal

Overview

Calcium is a chemical element with the symbol Ca and atomic number 20. It is a soft gray metal and the fifth most abundant element in the Earth's crust. Calcium is an essential component for living organisms, particularly in cell physiology, where calcium ions play a critical role in cellular signaling.

Discovered in 1808 by Sir Humphry Davy, calcium is widely used in various applications, including as a reducing agent in metal extraction and as a vital nutrient for bone health in humans and animals.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 20
  • Atomic Mass: 40.078 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Ar] 4s2
  • Group / Block: 2 (s-block), Alkaline Earth Metal
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 842 °C
  • Boiling Point: 1484 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Calcium is widely used in the construction industry, particularly in the form of calcium carbonate (limestone) for cement and concrete. It is also used in the steel and glass industries. In biology, calcium is a crucial component of bones and teeth, and its ions are involved in muscle function, nerve transmission, and blood clotting.

Calcium in Action

Calcium is a versatile element with a wide range of applications in industry, agriculture, and biology. It is essential for the growth and development of plants, animals, and humans, making it a vital element for life.

Historical Perspective

Calcium was first isolated in 1808 by Sir Humphry Davy using electrolysis. The name "calcium" is derived from the Latin word "calx," meaning lime, reflecting its discovery in compounds such as lime and limestone.

Today, calcium remains indispensable in construction, agriculture, and biological systems, highlighting its versatility and importance.
