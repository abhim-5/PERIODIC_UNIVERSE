Neptunium (Np)

Atomic Number 93 | A Rare and Radioactive Metal

Overview

Neptunium is a chemical element with the symbol Np and atomic number 93. It is a silvery, radioactive metal and the first transuranic element in the actinide series. Neptunium is primarily a byproduct of nuclear reactors and has limited practical uses.

Discovered in 1940 by Edwin McMillan and Philip Abelson, neptunium was named after the planet Neptune, following the naming convention of elements after planets.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 93
  • Atomic Mass: 237.05 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Rn] 5f4 6d1 7s2
  • Group / Block: Actinides (f-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 640 °C
  • Boiling Point: 3902 °C
  • Density: 20.45 g/cm³

Neptunium has limited applications, primarily in scientific research and as a precursor in the production of plutonium-238, which is used as a power source in spacecraft. Its radioactive properties are also studied for potential applications in nuclear energy.

Neptunium in Action

Historical Perspective

Neptunium was discovered in 1940 by Edwin McMillan and Philip Abelson at the University of California, Berkeley. Its discovery marked the beginning of the transuranic elements, expanding the periodic table beyond uranium.

While it has no widespread uses, neptunium's study contributes to our understanding of nuclear reactions and the synthesis of new elements.
