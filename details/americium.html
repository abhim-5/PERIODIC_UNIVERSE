Americium (Am)

Atomic Number 95 | A Synthetic Radioactive Element

Overview

Americium is a chemical element with the symbol Am and atomic number 95. It is a synthetic, silvery-white, radioactive metal belonging to the actinide series. Americium was first synthesized in 1944 by Glenn T. Seaborg, Ralph A. James, and Albert Ghiorso during the Manhattan Project.

Named after the Americas, americium has found applications in smoke detectors and as a neutron source for industrial and medical uses.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 95
  • Atomic Mass: 243 u (most stable isotope)
  • Electron Configuration: [Rn] 5f7 7s2
  • Group / Block: Actinides (f-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 1176 °C
  • Boiling Point: 2607 °C
  • Density: 12 g/cm³

Americium is widely used in commercial smoke detectors, where its alpha radiation ionizes air to detect smoke particles. It is also employed as a neutron source in industrial radiography and research applications.

Historical Perspective

Americium was first synthesized in 1944 by Glenn T. Seaborg and his team during the Manhattan Project. Its discovery marked a significant advancement in the study of transuranic elements.

Despite its radioactive nature, americium's unique properties have made it indispensable in smoke detection and neutron generation technologies.
