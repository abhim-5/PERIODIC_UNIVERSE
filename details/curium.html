Curium (Cm)

Atomic Number 96 | A Synthetic Radioactive Element

Overview

Curium is a chemical element with the symbol Cm and atomic number 96. It is a synthetic, radioactive metal belonging to the actinide series. Curium was first synthesized in 1944 by Glenn T. Seaborg, Ralph A. James, and Albert Ghiorso during the Manhattan Project.

Named in honor of Marie and Pierre Curie, curium is used in space missions as a power source and in neutron moisture gauges for soil analysis.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 96
  • Atomic Mass: 247 u (most stable isotope)
  • Electron Configuration: [Rn] 5f7 6d1 7s2
  • Group / Block: Actinides (f-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 1340 °C
  • Boiling Point: 3110 °C
  • Density: 13.51 g/cm³

Curium is used as a power source in space exploration, where its radioactive decay generates heat for thermoelectric generators. It is also employed in neutron moisture gauges and for research purposes in nuclear science.

Historical Perspective

Curium was first synthesized in 1944 by Glenn T. Seaborg and his team as part of the Manhattan Project. Its name honors the pioneering contributions of Marie and Pierre Curie to the field of radioactivity.

Despite its limited practical applications, curium remains an important element for research in nuclear chemistry and physics.
