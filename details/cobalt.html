Cobalt (Co)

Atomic Number 27 | Magnetic Transition Metal

Overview

Cobalt is a chemical element with the symbol Co and atomic number 27. Known for its magnetic properties, cobalt is a hard, lustrous, and silver-gray metal. It is widely used in alloys, rechargeable batteries, and as a pigment in ceramics and glass.

Cobalt was discovered in 1735 by Swedish chemist Georg Brandt, who identified it as a new element responsible for the blue color of glass. Today, cobalt is essential in various industrial and biological applications.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 27
  • Atomic Mass: 58.933 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Ar] 3d7 4s2
  • Group / Block: 9 (d-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 1495 °C
  • Boiling Point: 2927 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Cobalt is widely used in the production of high-strength alloys, rechargeable batteries, and magnetic materials. It is also used as a catalyst in chemical reactions and as a pigment to produce vibrant blue colors in ceramics and glass. In biology, cobalt is a key component of vitamin B12, essential for human health.

Cobalt in Action

Cobalt's unique properties make it a versatile element with a wide range of applications, from industrial processes to consumer products.

Historical Perspective

Cobalt was first identified as an element in 1735 by Georg Brandt, who showed that it was the source of the blue color in glass. Its name comes from the German word "kobalt" or "kobold," meaning goblin, as miners believed cobalt ores were cursed.

Today, cobalt plays a critical role in modern technology, particularly in the fields of energy storage and advanced materials.
