Radon (Rn)

Atomic Number 86 | Noble Gas and Health Hazard

Overview

Radon is a chemical element with the symbol Rn and atomic number 86. It is a radioactive noble gas that is colorless, odorless, and tasteless under standard conditions. Radon is formed as part of the natural radioactive decay chain of uranium and thorium.

Radon is of significant concern as a health hazard due to its radioactive properties. It can accumulate in poorly ventilated indoor spaces, leading to an increased risk of lung cancer upon prolonged exposure.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 86
  • Atomic Mass: 222 u (most stable isotope)
  • Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f14 5d10 6s2 6p6
  • Group / Block: 18 (p-block)
  • Standard State: Gas at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: −71 °C
  • Boiling Point: −61.7 °C
  • Radioactive: Yes

Radon has limited industrial applications due to its radioactivity. It has been used in radiation therapy for cancer treatment, though its use has declined with the development of safer alternatives. Monitoring radon levels in homes and buildings is critical to mitigate health risks.

Historical Perspective

Radon was discovered in 1900 by Friedrich Ernst Dorn while studying the decay of radium. Initially known as "radium emanation," its name was later changed to radon to reflect its origin.

The discovery of radon contributed to understanding the behavior of radioactive elements and their impact on health. Today, radon is a focus of public health efforts to minimize its presence in residential and occupational settings.
