Hydrogen (H)

Atomic Number 1 | The Lightest Element

Overview

Hydrogen is the first element in the periodic table, with the atomic symbol H and an atomic number of 1. It's the simplest and lightest of all elements, consisting of just one proton and one electron. Under standard conditions, hydrogen is a colorless, odorless, and highly flammable diatomic gas (H2).

Discovered as a distinct substance by Henry Cavendish in 1766, hydrogen is now recognized as the most abundant chemical substance in the universe, constituting roughly 75% of all baryonic mass.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 1
  • Atomic Mass: 1.008 u
  • Electron Configuration: 1s1
  • Group / Block: 1 (s-block)
  • Standard State: Gas at 25 °C
  • Boiling Point: −252.879 °C
  • Melting Point: −259.16 °C
  • Radioactive: No (most isotopes), but some isotopes like Tritium are radioactive.

Hydrogen has a wide range of applications, from serving as a rocket propellant to being used in fuel cells for clean energy production. It's also crucial in industrial processes (e.g., ammonia synthesis via the Haber process).

Historical Perspective

Hydrogen was first recognized as a discrete substance by Henry Cavendish in 1766. He described it as "inflammable air" which, when burned, formed water. Antoine Lavoisier later coined the name "hydrogen" from Greek roots meaning "water-former."

Over centuries, hydrogen’s role in chemistry and industry has expanded dramatically. From the iconic demonstration of the Hindenburg airship in 1937 to the modern push for hydrogen fuel cells in automobiles, the story of hydrogen is one of discovery, triumph, and caution.
