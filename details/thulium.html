Thulium (Tm)

Atomic Number 69 | A Rare Earth Metal with Specialized Uses

Overview

Thulium is a chemical element with the symbol Tm and atomic number 69. It is a soft, silvery-gray metal that belongs to the lanthanide series of rare earth elements. Thulium is relatively rare and has unique properties that make it useful in specific applications.

Discovered in 1879 by Swedish chemist Per Teodor Cleve, thulium was named after "Thule," a mythical land believed to be the northernmost region of the ancient world.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 69
  • Atomic Mass: 168.93 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f13 6s2
  • Group / Block: Lanthanides (f-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 1545 °C
  • Boiling Point: 1950 °C
  • Density: 9.32 g/cm³

Thulium is used in portable X-ray devices and in certain high-temperature superconductors. It is also employed as a dopant in fiber lasers and solid-state devices, enhancing their efficiency and performance.

Historical Perspective

Thulium was discovered in 1879 by Per Teodor Cleve, who identified it while analyzing the mineral erbia. Its name, inspired by the mythical land of Thule, reflects the element's Nordic discovery.

Today, thulium’s applications in niche technologies, such as lasers and portable X-ray machines, make it a valuable material in scientific research and medical devices.
