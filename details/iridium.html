Iridium (Ir)

Atomic Number 77 | Corrosion-Resistant Transition Metal

Overview

Iridium is a chemical element with the symbol Ir and atomic number 77. It is a hard, brittle, silvery-white transition metal in the platinum group, known for its extreme resistance to corrosion and high density. Iridium is one of the rarest elements in the Earth's crust.

Discovered in 1803 by Smithson Tennant, iridium was named after the Latin word "iris," meaning rainbow, due to the colorful nature of its salts. Its unique properties make it invaluable in industrial and scientific applications.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 77
  • Atomic Mass: 192.22 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f14 5d7 6s2
  • Group / Block: 9 (d-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 2446 °C
  • Boiling Point: 4428 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Iridium is primarily used in high-temperature and corrosion-resistant materials, such as spark plugs, crucibles, and electrodes. It is also employed in the production of specialized alloys and in scientific instruments. Iridium is a critical material in the aerospace industry for coating parts exposed to extreme conditions.

Historical Perspective

Iridium was discovered in 1803 by Smithson Tennant during his analysis of platinum ores. Its name reflects the vivid colors of its compounds. Due to its rarity and remarkable properties, iridium has been used in various high-value applications since its discovery.

Today, iridium plays a crucial role in advanced technologies, from medical devices to deep-space missions, where durability and resistance to extreme conditions are essential.
