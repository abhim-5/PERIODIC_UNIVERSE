Nickel (Ni)

Atomic Number 28 | Durable and Corrosion-Resistant Metal

Overview

Nickel is a chemical element with the symbol Ni and atomic number 28. Known for its high corrosion resistance and strength, nickel is a silver-white metal widely used in alloys, electroplating, and as a key component in rechargeable batteries.

First isolated in 1751 by Axel Fredrik Cronstedt, nickel has become a crucial element in various industries, from manufacturing to energy storage.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 28
  • Atomic Mass: 58.693 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Ar] 3d8 4s2
  • Group / Block: 10 (d-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 1455 °C
  • Boiling Point: 2913 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Nickel is used extensively in stainless steel production, where it enhances corrosion resistance and strength. It is also a key material in rechargeable batteries, including lithium-ion batteries, and in electroplating to protect other metals from corrosion. Nickel alloys are used in aerospace and marine environments due to their durability.

Historical Perspective

Nickel was first identified as an element in 1751 by Swedish chemist Axel Fredrik Cronstedt, who isolated it from the mineral niccolite. Its name is derived from the German word "kupfernickel," meaning "devil's copper," as miners believed it was a copper ore that refused to yield copper.

Today, nickel plays a vital role in modern technology, from renewable energy systems to advanced manufacturing processes.
