Antimony (Sb)

Atomic Number 51 | Unique Metalloid

Overview

Antimony is a chemical element with the symbol Sb (from Latin: stibium) and atomic number 51. It is a lustrous gray metalloid that exhibits properties of both metals and nonmetals. Antimony is primarily used in alloys to improve hardness and strength.

Known since ancient times, antimony has been used in cosmetics and medicines. Today, it finds applications in flame retardants, semiconductors, and as a catalyst in industrial processes.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 51
  • Atomic Mass: 121.76 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Kr] 4d10 5s2 5p3
  • Group / Block: 15 (p-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 630.63 °C
  • Boiling Point: 1587 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Antimony is commonly used in flame retardants to enhance safety in textiles and plastics. It is also employed in lead-acid batteries, ceramics, and glass production. Antimony compounds are critical in the semiconductor industry for making diodes and infrared detectors.

Historical Perspective

Antimony has been known since ancient times and was used in cosmetics, such as kohl eyeliner. Its name is derived from the Greek words "anti" and "monos," meaning "not alone," reflecting its occurrence with other elements. It gained industrial significance in the 20th century with the rise of flame-retardant technologies.

Today, antimony is a critical material in industries ranging from electronics to safety equipment, demonstrating its versatility and importance.
