Xenon (Xe)

Atomic Number 54 | Noble Gas

Overview

Xenon is a chemical element with the symbol Xe and atomic number 54. It is a colorless, dense, odorless noble gas found in trace amounts in the Earth's atmosphere. Xenon is notable for its use in lighting and as an anesthetic.

Discovered in 1898 by Sir William Ramsay and Morris Travers, xenon is named after the Greek word "xenos," meaning "strange." Despite its rarity, xenon has unique applications in medicine, technology, and science.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 54
  • Atomic Mass: 131.29 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Kr] 4d10 5s2 5p6
  • Group / Block: 18 (p-block)
  • Standard State: Gas at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: −111.8 °C
  • Boiling Point: −108.1 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Xenon is widely used in high-intensity discharge lamps, including automotive headlights and stadium lighting. It is also used as an anesthetic and in ion propulsion systems for spacecraft. In scientific research, xenon isotopes are used in imaging and as tracers in nuclear medicine.

Historical Perspective

Xenon was discovered in 1898 by Sir William Ramsay and Morris Travers during their study of the components of liquid air. Its discovery added to the understanding of noble gases and their properties. Over time, xenon's unique characteristics have been harnessed in various high-tech applications.

Today, xenon is a critical material in lighting, medical imaging, and space exploration technologies.
