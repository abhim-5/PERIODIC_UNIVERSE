Argon (Ar)

Atomic Number 18 | Noble Gas

Overview

Argon is a chemical element with the symbol Ar and atomic number 18. It is a colorless, odorless noble gas that is the third most abundant gas in the Earth's atmosphere, making up about 0.93% by volume. Argon is inert under most conditions and has a variety of industrial uses.

Discovered in 1894 by Lord Rayleigh and Sir William Ramsay, argon gets its name from the Greek word "argos," meaning "lazy," due to its low chemical reactivity.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 18
  • Atomic Mass: 39.948 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Ne] 3s2 3p6
  • Group / Block: 18 (p-block)
  • Standard State: Gas at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: −189.3 °C
  • Boiling Point: −185.8 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Argon is widely used as an inert shielding gas in welding and other high-temperature industrial processes where materials are sensitive to oxidation. It is also used in lighting, such as in neon signs and incandescent light bulbs, where it prevents filament degradation. Additionally, argon is utilized in the preservation of historical documents and in the manufacturing of silicon and germanium crystals.

Historical Perspective

Argon was discovered in 1894 by Lord Rayleigh and Sir William Ramsay while studying the composition of air. Its discovery marked the first identification of a noble gas in the atmosphere. The element's name reflects its chemical inertness.

Today, argon is an essential component in various scientific, industrial, and medical applications. Its inert nature makes it invaluable in processes where reactivity must be minimized.
