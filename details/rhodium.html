Rhodium (Rh)

Atomic Number 45 | Lustrous Platinum Group Metal

Overview

Rhodium is a chemical element with the symbol Rh and atomic number 45. It is a rare, silvery-white transition metal that belongs to the platinum group of the periodic table. Known for its reflective properties and resistance to corrosion, rhodium is widely used in catalytic converters, jewelry, and mirrors.

Discovered in 1803 by William Hyde Wollaston, rhodium gets its name from the Greek word "rhodon," meaning rose, due to the rose-red color of its compounds. Despite its rarity, it is an essential material in industry.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 45
  • Atomic Mass: 102.91 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Kr] 4d8 5s1
  • Group / Block: 9 (d-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 1964 °C
  • Boiling Point: 3695 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Rhodium is primarily used in catalytic converters for reducing harmful vehicle emissions. It is also utilized in jewelry plating to enhance durability and luster. Rhodium mirrors are employed in high-precision optical instruments, and its compounds serve as catalysts in chemical reactions.

Historical Perspective

Rhodium was discovered in 1803 by British chemist William Hyde Wollaston while analyzing platinum ore. Its unique reflective and corrosion-resistant properties quickly made it a valuable material for industrial applications.

Today, rhodium is considered one of the most expensive metals due to its rarity and widespread use in pollution control technologies.
