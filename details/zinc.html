Zinc (Zn)

Atomic Number 30 | Essential Transition Metal

Overview

Zinc is a chemical element with the symbol Zn and atomic number 30. It is a bluish-white, lustrous metal that is widely used in galvanizing to protect steel and iron from rusting. Zinc is essential for human health, playing a key role in various enzymatic processes.

Known since ancient times, zinc was first isolated as a pure element in the 13th century in India. Its widespread use spans industries including construction, healthcare, and electronics.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 30
  • Atomic Mass: 65.38 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Ar] 3d10 4s2
  • Group / Block: 12 (d-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 419.53 °C
  • Boiling Point: 907 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Zinc is commonly used for galvanizing steel and iron to prevent corrosion. It is also a vital component in batteries, particularly alkaline batteries, and in the production of brass, an alloy of copper and zinc. In biology, zinc is an essential trace element required for immune function and enzyme activity.

Historical Perspective

Zinc has been used since ancient times in the form of alloys like brass. It was first recognized as a distinct element in India during the 13th century. Its name is derived from the German word "zinke," meaning prong or spike, due to the shape of zinc crystals.

Today, zinc continues to be a vital industrial and biological material, with applications ranging from construction to medicine.
