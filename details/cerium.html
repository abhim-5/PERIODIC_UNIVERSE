Cerium (Ce)

Atomic Number 58 | A Lanthanide with Versatile Applications

Overview

Cerium is a chemical element with the symbol Ce and atomic number 58. It is a soft, silvery-white metal and is the most abundant of the lanthanides. Cerium is reactive, tarnishing in air and burning readily when heated.

Discovered in 1803 by Martin Heinrich Klaproth and Jöns Jakob Berzelius, cerium was named after the dwarf planet Ceres. It is widely used in industrial applications due to its chemical versatility.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 58
  • Atomic Mass: 140.116 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f1 5d1 6s2
  • Group / Block: Lanthanides (f-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 798 °C
  • Boiling Point: 3443 °C
  • Density: 6.77 g/cm³

Cerium is widely used in catalytic converters for automobiles, as it helps to reduce emissions. It is also used in glass polishing powders, flints for lighters, and as an additive in alloys to improve strength and resistance to corrosion.

Historical Perspective

Cerium was independently discovered in 1803 by scientists Martin Heinrich Klaproth, Jöns Jakob Berzelius, and Wilhelm Hisinger. The element was named after the dwarf planet Ceres, which had been discovered just two years earlier.

Over time, cerium has found a wide range of uses, from glass polishing and catalytic converters to advances in materials science. Its role in modern industry underscores its versatility and importance.
