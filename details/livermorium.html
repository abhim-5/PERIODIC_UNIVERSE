Livermorium (Lv)

Atomic Number 116 | Synthetic and Highly Radioactive Element

Overview

Livermorium is a chemical element with the symbol Lv and atomic number 116. It is a synthetic element that is not found naturally and is produced in particle accelerators. Livermorium is highly unstable, with its most stable isotope, Lv-293, having a half-life of about 60 milliseconds.

Livermorium was first synthesized in 2000 by a joint team of Russian and American scientists at the Joint Institute for Nuclear Research (JINR) in Dubna, Russia. The element is named after the Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory in Livermore, California, in recognition of its contributions to superheavy element research.

Properties & Uses

Due to its short half-life and instability, livermorium has no practical applications. It is used exclusively in scientific research to study the properties of superheavy elements and to expand our understanding of the periodic table.

Historical Perspective

Livermorium was first synthesized in 2000 by a team led by Yuri Oganessian. Its name honors the Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory for its pioneering work in the field of superheavy element research.

Although it has no practical applications, the discovery of livermorium contributes to the study of superheavy elements and enhances our understanding of atomic structure and stability.
