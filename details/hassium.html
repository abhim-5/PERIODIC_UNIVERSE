Hassium (Hs)

Atomic Number 108 | Synthetic and Highly Radioactive Element

Overview

Hassium is a chemical element with the symbol Hs and atomic number 108. It is a synthetic element that is not found naturally and is produced in particle accelerators by bombarding lighter elements with heavy ions. Its most stable isotope, Hs-277, has a half-life of approximately 11 minutes.

Hassium was first synthesized in 1984 by a German research team at the GSI Helmholtz Centre for Heavy Ion Research. The element is named after the German state of Hesse, where the research center is located.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 108
  • Atomic Mass: [277] u (most stable isotope)
  • Electron Configuration: [Rn] 5f14 6d6 7s2
  • Group / Block: 8 (d-block)
  • Standard State: Solid (predicted)
  • Melting Point: Unknown
  • Boiling Point: Unknown
  • Radioactive: Yes

Due to its short half-life and instability, hassium has no practical applications. It is used exclusively for scientific research to study the properties of superheavy elements and to advance our understanding of the periodic table.

Historical Perspective

Hassium was first synthesized in 1984 by a German research team led by Peter Armbruster and Gottfried Münzenberg. Its name was derived from the state of Hesse in Germany, honoring the location of its discovery. The element’s discovery was a significant milestone in the study of superheavy elements.

Research on hassium continues to explore its nuclear properties and its position in the periodic table. Although it has no practical applications, its study contributes to our understanding of chemical and physical behaviors in extreme conditions.
