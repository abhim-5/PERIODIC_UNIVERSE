Promethium (Pm)

Atomic Number 61 | A Rare and Radioactive Lanthanide

Overview

Promethium is a chemical element with the symbol Pm and atomic number 61. It is a rare, radioactive metal that belongs to the lanthanide series. Promethium is unique among the lanthanides because it has no stable isotopes.

Discovered in 1945 by Jacob A. Marinsky, Lawrence E. Glendenin, and Charles D. Coryell, promethium was named after Prometheus, the Titan in Greek mythology who stole fire from the gods to give to humanity.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 61
  • Atomic Mass: 145 u (most stable isotope)
  • Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f5 6s2
  • Group / Block: Lanthanides (f-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 1042 °C
  • Boiling Point: 3000 °C
  • Density: 7.26 g/cm³

Promethium has limited applications due to its radioactivity. It is used in luminous paint, atomic batteries, and as a beta radiation source in scientific research and technology.

Historical Perspective

Promethium was first isolated and identified in 1945 from uranium fission products. Its discovery was significant because it filled a gap in the periodic table.

Despite its rarity, promethium has found niche applications in devices requiring a lightweight and portable energy source, such as in space and military technologies.
