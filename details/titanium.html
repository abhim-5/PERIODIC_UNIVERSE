Titanium (Ti)

Atomic Number 22 | Strong and Lightweight Metal

Overview

Titanium is a chemical element with the symbol Ti and atomic number 22. Known for its high strength-to-weight ratio, corrosion resistance, and biocompatibility, titanium is widely used in aerospace, medical, and industrial applications.

Discovered in 1791 by William Gregor, titanium is the ninth most abundant element in the Earth's crust and is commonly found in minerals such as ilmenite and rutile.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 22
  • Atomic Mass: 47.867 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Ar] 3d2 4s2
  • Group / Block: 4 (d-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 1668 °C
  • Boiling Point: 3287 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Titanium is widely used in the production of aircraft and spacecraft due to its lightweight and durable properties. It is also used in medical implants, chemical processing equipment, and consumer products such as bicycles and golf clubs. Titanium dioxide, a compound of titanium, is a key ingredient in paints, coatings, and sunscreens.

Titanium in Action

Titanium is a versatile metal with a wide range of applications, from aerospace engineering to medical devices. Its unique combination of strength, lightness, and resistance to corrosion makes it an essential material in modern technology.

Historical Perspective

Titanium was first identified in 1791 by British geologist William Gregor in the mineral ilmenite. Its name, given by Martin Heinrich Klaproth, comes from the Titans of Greek mythology, reflecting its strong and enduring properties.

The metal gained industrial importance in the 20th century, particularly in the aerospace industry, and remains a critical material in advanced engineering and technology.
