Yttrium (Y)

Atomic Number 39 | Transition Metal

Overview

Yttrium is a chemical element with the symbol Y and atomic number 39. It is a silvery-metallic transition metal that is commonly found in rare-earth minerals. Yttrium is used in various applications, including phosphors, ceramics, and superconductors.

Discovered in 1794 by Johan Gadolin, yttrium is named after the village of Ytterby in Sweden, where its ore was first identified. Today, yttrium compounds play a crucial role in modern technologies.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 39
  • Atomic Mass: 88.905 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Kr] 4d1 5s2
  • Group / Block: 3 (d-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 1526 °C
  • Boiling Point: 3336 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Yttrium is widely used in the production of LEDs and phosphors for color televisions and displays. It is also a key component in superconductors, ceramics, and certain alloys to improve strength and durability. Yttrium-aluminum garnet (YAG) is used in lasers and as a gemstone.

Historical Perspective

Yttrium was first isolated in 1828 by Friedrich Wöhler. Its compounds were initially used in gas mantles and later became crucial in the development of color television technology. The village of Ytterby, where it was discovered, is also associated with other rare-earth elements.

Today, yttrium is a vital material in high-tech applications, including renewable energy systems and medical imaging.
