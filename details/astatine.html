Astatine (At)

Atomic Number 85 | Rare and Highly Radioactive Halogen

Overview

Astatine is a chemical element with the symbol At and atomic number 85. It is a rare, radioactive halogen that is the heaviest member of the halogen group. Astatine occurs in nature only as a result of decay of heavier elements and is extremely scarce.

Discovered in 1940 by Dale R. Corson, Kenneth Ross MacKenzie, and Emilio Segrè, astatine’s name comes from the Greek word "astatos," meaning "unstable." Due to its short half-life and rarity, astatine has limited practical applications.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 85
  • Atomic Mass: 210 u (most stable isotope)
  • Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f14 5d10 6s2 6p5
  • Group / Block: 17 (p-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 302 °C
  • Boiling Point: 337 °C
  • Radioactive: Yes

Astatine has been studied for its potential use in targeted alpha-particle radiotherapy for cancer treatment. However, its scarcity and radioactivity make it challenging to handle and study extensively.

Historical Perspective

Astatine was first synthesized in 1940 by bombarding bismuth with alpha particles in a cyclotron. Its discovery added to the understanding of radioactive elements and their behaviors. The name "astatine" reflects its instability, which is a defining characteristic of the element.

Due to its rarity and the difficulty of isolating it, astatine remains a scientific curiosity rather than a widely-used element. Research continues into its potential medical applications, particularly in radiotherapy.
