Rubidium (Rb)

Atomic Number 37 | Highly Reactive Alkali Metal

Overview

Rubidium is a chemical element with the symbol Rb and atomic number 37. It is a soft, silvery-white alkali metal that is highly reactive and flammable. Rubidium is found in minerals such as lepidolite and pollucite, though it is not as abundant as some other alkali metals.

Discovered in 1861 by Robert Bunsen and Gustav Kirchhoff using flame spectroscopy, rubidium is named after the Latin word "rubidus," meaning deep red, referring to the red lines in its emission spectrum.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 37
  • Atomic Mass: 85.4678 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Kr] 5s1
  • Group / Block: 1 (s-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 39.31 °C
  • Boiling Point: 688 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Rubidium is primarily used in research and development, particularly in atomic physics and spectroscopy. It is also used in specialized glasses, electronics, and in the preparation of rubidium salts. In recent years, rubidium has been studied for its potential applications in ion engines and atomic clocks.

Historical Perspective

Rubidium was discovered in 1861 by Robert Bunsen and Gustav Kirchhoff during their investigation of mineral samples. Its unique red spectral lines led to its naming and identification as a new element. Though rubidium is not widely used, its role in scientific research continues to grow.

Today, rubidium is considered a critical material for advanced technologies, including quantum computing and timekeeping.
