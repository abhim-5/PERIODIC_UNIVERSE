Bromine (Br)

Atomic Number 35 | Reactive Halogen

Overview

Bromine is a chemical element with the symbol Br and atomic number 35. It is a reddish-brown liquid at room temperature, making it one of the only two elements that are liquid in their standard state. Bromine is highly reactive and belongs to the halogen group.

Discovered in 1826 by Antoine Jérôme Balard, bromine has various industrial uses, including flame retardants, water treatment, and as a precursor in chemical synthesis.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 35
  • Atomic Mass: 79.904 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Ar] 3d10 4s2 4p5
  • Group / Block: 17 (p-block)
  • Standard State: Liquid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: −7.2 °C
  • Boiling Point: 58.8 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Bromine is widely used in the production of flame retardants and water disinfectants. It also plays a role in the pharmaceutical industry and in the manufacture of photographic chemicals. Bromine compounds are essential for various applications, ranging from dye production to drilling fluids.

Historical Perspective

Bromine was discovered in 1826 by French chemist Antoine Jérôme Balard while analyzing salt water. Its name is derived from the Greek word "bromos," meaning stench, due to its strong and unpleasant smell. Bromine has played a significant role in industrial and pharmaceutical applications since its discovery.

Today, bromine remains an important element in flame retardant technologies and water purification processes.
