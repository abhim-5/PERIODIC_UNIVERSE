Einsteinium (Es)

Atomic Number 99 | A Rare Synthetic Radioactive Element

Overview

Einsteinium is a chemical element with the symbol Es and atomic number 99. It is a synthetic, radioactive metal in the actinide series. Einsteinium was first identified in 1952 in the debris of the first hydrogen bomb test, Ivy Mike, conducted in the Pacific Ocean.

Named after Albert Einstein, einsteinium is primarily used for scientific research, particularly in the synthesis of other elements.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 99
  • Atomic Mass: 252 u (most stable isotope)
  • Electron Configuration: [Rn] 5f11 7s2
  • Group / Block: Actinides (f-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 860 °C
  • Boiling Point: Unknown
  • Density: 8.84 g/cm³

Einsteinium is used primarily in scientific research. Its isotopes have been used in the synthesis of other transuranium elements, such as mendelevium. Due to its high radioactivity, its practical applications are extremely limited.

Historical Perspective

Einsteinium was discovered in 1952 by a team of scientists analyzing debris from the first hydrogen bomb test. Its name honors Albert Einstein for his contributions to physics and science.

Today, einsteinium remains a focus of scientific research, helping to expand our understanding of heavy elements and nuclear chemistry.
