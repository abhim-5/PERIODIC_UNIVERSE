Uranium (U)

Atomic Number 92 | A Heavy Metal Used in Nuclear Energy

Overview

Uranium is a chemical element with the symbol U and atomic number 92. It is a heavy, silvery-gray metal that is weakly radioactive and belongs to the actinide series. Uranium is a key material in nuclear reactors and atomic bombs.

Discovered in 1789 by German chemist Martin Heinrich Klaproth, uranium was named after the planet Uranus. Its unique properties make it one of the most significant elements for nuclear energy.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 92
  • Atomic Mass: 238.03 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Rn] 5f3 6d1 7s2
  • Group / Block: Actinides (f-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 1132.2 °C
  • Boiling Point: 4131 °C
  • Density: 19.1 g/cm³

Uranium is primarily used as fuel in nuclear reactors to generate electricity. Its isotopes, particularly U-235, are crucial in both civilian and military applications, including atomic bombs and nuclear propulsion systems.

Historical Perspective

Uranium was first isolated in 1841 by Eugène-Melchior Péligot. Its radioactive properties were later discovered in 1896 by Henri Becquerel, marking a significant milestone in the study of radioactivity.

Today, uranium plays a pivotal role in the global energy landscape, with its use in nuclear power offering a low-carbon alternative to fossil fuels.
