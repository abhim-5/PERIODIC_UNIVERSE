Lutetium (Lu)

Atomic Number 71 | A Rare Earth Metal with High Density

Overview

Lutetium is a chemical element with the symbol Lu and atomic number 71. It is a silvery-white, hard metal that belongs to the lanthanide series of rare earth elements. Lutetium is the densest and hardest of the lanthanides, making it valuable for various specialized applications.

Discovered in 1907 independently by Georges Urbain in France and Carl Auer von Welsbach in Austria, lutetium was named after Lutetia, the ancient Roman name for Paris.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 71
  • Atomic Mass: 174.97 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f14 5d1 6s2
  • Group / Block: Lanthanides (f-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 1663 °C
  • Boiling Point: 3402 °C
  • Density: 9.84 g/cm³

Lutetium is used in cancer treatment as a radioactive isotope in targeted radiotherapy. It is also employed as a catalyst in petroleum refining and as a dopant in high-performance optical materials.

Historical Perspective

Lutetium was independently discovered in 1907 by Georges Urbain and Carl Auer von Welsbach during their studies of rare earth oxides. Its name reflects its Parisian origins, derived from Lutetia, the ancient Roman name for the city.

Today, lutetium’s role in advanced technologies, including medical imaging and industrial catalysts, underscores its scientific and industrial importance.
