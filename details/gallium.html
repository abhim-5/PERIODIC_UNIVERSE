Gallium (Ga)

Atomic Number 31 | Unique Metal with Low Melting Point

Overview

Gallium is a chemical element with the symbol Ga and atomic number 31. It is a soft, silvery metal that melts just above room temperature, making it unique among metals. Gallium is used in semiconductors, LEDs, and solar panels due to its ability to form compounds with high electrical conductivity.

Discovered in 1875 by Paul-Émile Lecoq de Boisbaudran, gallium has gained importance in modern electronics and photonics industries.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 31
  • Atomic Mass: 69.723 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Ar] 3d10 4s2 4p1
  • Group / Block: 13 (p-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 29.76 °C
  • Boiling Point: 2204 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Gallium is extensively used in semiconductors and electronic devices, particularly in gallium arsenide (GaAs) and gallium nitride (GaN) components. It is also utilized in solar panels, thermometers, and as a coolant in some nuclear reactors.

Historical Perspective

Gallium was discovered in 1875 by Paul-Émile Lecoq de Boisbaudran while analyzing sphalerite. Its name is derived from "Gallia," the Latin name for France. Gallium's unusual melting properties and ability to form compounds with unique electronic properties have made it a cornerstone of modern electronics.

Today, gallium is critical in emerging technologies such as high-efficiency solar cells and advanced LEDs.
