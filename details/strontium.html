Strontium (Sr)

Atomic Number 38 | Soft Alkaline Earth Metal

Overview

Strontium is a chemical element with the symbol Sr and atomic number 38. It is a soft, silvery-white metal that belongs to the alkaline earth metals group. Strontium is highly reactive, especially with water, and it must be stored in kerosene or a similar substance to prevent oxidation.

Discovered in 1790 by Adair Crawford, strontium was named after the Scottish village of Strontian, where its ore was first identified. Strontium is often used in fireworks for its bright red flame and in other industrial applications.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 38
  • Atomic Mass: 87.62 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Kr] 5s2
  • Group / Block: 2 (s-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 777 °C
  • Boiling Point: 1377 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Strontium is commonly used in the production of fireworks and flares, as its salts produce a bright red color. It is also used in the glass for cathode ray tubes and in the refining of sugar from sugar beet. Additionally, the radioactive isotope strontium-90 is used in medical and scientific applications, including cancer treatment.

Historical Perspective

Strontium was first isolated as a pure element in 1808 by Humphry Davy through electrolysis. Its bright red flame in fireworks has been known since the early 19th century, making it a popular component in pyrotechnics.

Today, strontium is studied for its potential applications in energy storage and biomedical fields, highlighting its versatility as a material.
