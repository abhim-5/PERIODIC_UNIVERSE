Zirconium (Zr)

Atomic Number 40 | Strong and Corrosion-Resistant Metal

Overview

Zirconium is a chemical element with the symbol Zr and atomic number 40. It is a lustrous, gray-white, strong transition metal that is highly resistant to corrosion. Zirconium is commonly found in the mineral zircon and is used extensively in nuclear reactors due to its low neutron absorption.

Discovered in 1789 by Martin Heinrich Klaproth, zirconium is named after the mineral zircon, from which it was first isolated. Today, it is an important material in aerospace, medical, and industrial applications.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 40
  • Atomic Mass: 91.224 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Kr] 4d2 5s2
  • Group / Block: 4 (d-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 1855 °C
  • Boiling Point: 4409 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Zirconium is widely used in the cladding of nuclear reactor fuel rods due to its low neutron absorption cross-section. It is also used in the production of heat-resistant ceramics, surgical instruments, and as an alloying agent to improve the strength and corrosion resistance of other metals. Zirconium dioxide (zirconia) is used in dental and orthopedic implants.

Historical Perspective

Zirconium was first identified as an element in 1789 by Martin Heinrich Klaproth while analyzing the mineral zircon. It was first isolated as a pure metal in 1824 by Jöns Jakob Berzelius. Zirconium's exceptional resistance to heat and corrosion makes it a critical material in high-performance environments.

Today, zirconium continues to play a key role in nuclear energy, aerospace engineering, and advanced medical technologies.
