Indium (In)

Atomic Number 49 | Soft and Ductile Post-Transition Metal

Overview

Indium is a chemical element with the symbol In and atomic number 49. It is a soft, silvery-white metal that is malleable, ductile, and has a low melting point. Indium is commonly used in electronics, particularly in touchscreens, LCDs, and solders.

Discovered in 1863 by Ferdinand Reich and Hieronymous Theodor Richter, indium gets its name from the indigo blue line in its emission spectrum. Despite being relatively rare, it has become essential in modern technology.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 49
  • Atomic Mass: 114.82 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Kr] 4d10 5s2 5p1
  • Group / Block: 13 (p-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 156.6 °C
  • Boiling Point: 2072 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Indium is widely used in the production of indium tin oxide (ITO), which is essential for touchscreens and LCDs. It is also employed in solders, semiconductors, and in the coatings of aircraft engines to protect against corrosion. Its ability to bond to glass and create a conductive layer makes it vital in the electronics industry.

Historical Perspective

Indium was discovered in 1863 during the analysis of zinc ores. Its discovery was marked by the presence of a distinct indigo line in its spectrum. Initially considered a laboratory curiosity, indium's practical uses emerged with advancements in electronics and materials science.

Today, indium is a critical material in technologies such as flat-panel displays, solar panels, and advanced coatings.
