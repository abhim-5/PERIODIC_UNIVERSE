Molybdenum (Mo)

Atomic Number 42 | Essential Transition Metal

Overview

Molybdenum is a chemical element with the symbol Mo and atomic number 42. It is a silvery-gray metal known for its high melting point, strength, and resistance to corrosion. Molybdenum is widely used in steel alloys to enhance strength and high-temperature performance.

Discovered in 1778 by Carl Wilhelm Scheele, molybdenum is an essential trace element in biological systems, playing a vital role in enzyme function. Its industrial importance has made it a key material in modern technologies.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 42
  • Atomic Mass: 95.95 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Kr] 4d5 5s1
  • Group / Block: 6 (d-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 2623 °C
  • Boiling Point: 4639 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Molybdenum is used in steel alloys to improve hardness and resistance to corrosion. It is also employed in high-temperature environments, such as in jet engines and gas turbines. Additionally, molybdenum compounds are used in catalysts for the petroleum industry and as pigments in ceramics and paints.

Molybdenum in Action

Molybdenum is a versatile element with a wide range of applications, from strengthening steel to supporting biological processes. Its unique properties make it an essential component in various industries, including aerospace, energy, and catalysis.

Historical Perspective

Molybdenum was first recognized as an element in 1778 by Carl Wilhelm Scheele while analyzing molybdenite. Its name comes from the Greek word "molybdos," meaning lead, due to its similar appearance. It was later isolated as a pure metal in 1781 by Peter Jacob Hjelm.

Today, molybdenum is indispensable in metallurgy, renewable energy technologies, and biological research, underscoring its importance in both industry and science.
