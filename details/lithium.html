Lithium (Li)

Atomic Number 3 | Lightest Metal

Overview

Lithium is a chemical element with the symbol Li and atomic number 3. It is a soft, silvery-white alkali metal. Under standard conditions, it is the lightest metal and the least dense solid element.

Like all alkali metals, lithium is highly reactive and flammable, and must be stored in mineral oil or inert atmospheres to prevent oxidation. It has important industrial applications, from lithium-ion batteries to medicine.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 3
  • Atomic Mass: 6.94 u
  • Electron Configuration: [He] 2s1
  • Group / Block: Alkali Metal (s-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 180.5 °C
  • Boiling Point: 1342 °C
  • Radioactive: No (stable isotopes exist), but some isotopes can be radioactive.

Lithium’s low density, high electrochemical potential, and reactivity make it extremely important in battery technology (Li-ion batteries). It's also used in certain medications to treat bipolar disorder, as well as in alloys to improve strength and weight characteristics.

Historical Perspective

Lithium was first isolated in 1821 by William Thomas Brande, though it was initially discovered in mineral form by Johan August Arfwedson in 1817. Arfwedson found the new element in petalite ore, but it took years until pure elemental lithium was successfully produced.

In the 20th century, lithium found increasing use, starting with lithium-based lubricating greases. Today, lithium’s importance in battery technology, electronics, and even medicine underscores its growing role in modern society.
