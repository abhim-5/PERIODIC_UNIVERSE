Lead (Pb)

Atomic Number 82 | Dense and Versatile Post-Transition Metal

Overview

Lead is a chemical element with the symbol Pb (from Latin: plumbum) and atomic number 82. It is a soft, heavy, and malleable metal that has been used by humans for thousands of years. Lead is known for its high density and resistance to corrosion.

Historically used in pipes, paints, and fuels, lead's toxicity has led to strict regulations on its use. Despite this, it remains important in batteries, radiation shielding, and other specialized applications.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 82
  • Atomic Mass: 207.2 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f14 5d10 6s2 6p2
  • Group / Block: 14 (p-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 327.46 °C
  • Boiling Point: 1749 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Lead is primarily used in lead-acid batteries, which power vehicles and provide backup energy storage. It is also employed in radiation shielding for medical and industrial applications. Other uses include weights, solders, and in the production of certain alloys.

Lead in Action

Historical Perspective

Lead has been used since ancient times, with its earliest known applications in Roman plumbing systems and decorative items. Its malleability and abundance made it a popular material throughout history. In the modern era, the health risks of lead exposure have led to reduced usage and increased regulation.

Despite these limitations, lead remains a valuable resource in batteries, radiation shielding, and industrial manufacturing.
