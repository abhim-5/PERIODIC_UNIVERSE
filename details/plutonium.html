Plutonium (Pu)

Atomic Number 94 | A Radioactive Element Used in Nuclear Energy

Overview

Plutonium is a chemical element with the symbol Pu and atomic number 94. It is a dense, silvery-gray metal that is highly radioactive. Plutonium is a key material in nuclear reactors and nuclear weapons.

Discovered in 1940 by Glenn T. Seaborg, Edwin McMillan, Joseph W. Kennedy, and Arthur Wahl, plutonium was named after the dwarf planet Pluto, following the tradition of naming elements after celestial bodies.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 94
  • Atomic Mass: 244 u (most stable isotope)
  • Electron Configuration: [Rn] 5f6 7s2
  • Group / Block: Actinides (f-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 640 °C
  • Boiling Point: 3228 °C
  • Density: 19.84 g/cm³

Plutonium is used in nuclear reactors and as fuel for spacecraft. Its isotope, Pu-239, is critical for the production of nuclear weapons. Plutonium also has applications in radiothermal generators, which provide long-lasting energy for space missions.

Historical Perspective

Plutonium was first synthesized in 1940 by a team led by Glenn T. Seaborg. Its discovery expanded the periodic table and played a pivotal role in the development of nuclear energy and weapons during the 20th century.

Today, plutonium continues to be a critical material in nuclear technology, offering both opportunities and challenges in energy and security.
