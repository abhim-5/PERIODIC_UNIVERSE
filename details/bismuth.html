Bismuth (Bi)

Atomic Number 83 | Heavy Metal with Beautiful Colors

Overview

Bismuth is a chemical element with the symbol Bi and atomic number 83. It is a heavy, brittle metal with a silvery-white appearance, often showing an iridescent colorful oxidation surface. Bismuth is the most naturally diamagnetic element, and among the least thermally conductive of metals.

Known since ancient times, bismuth was often mistaken for lead or tin due to its similar properties. Modern uses of bismuth include cosmetics, pigments, and pharmaceuticals, such as the popular bismuth subsalicylate (the active ingredient in Pepto-Bismol).

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 83
  • Atomic Mass: 208.980 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f14 5d10 6s2 6p3
  • Group / Block: 15 (p-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 271.4 °C
  • Boiling Point: 1564 °C
  • Radioactive: Yes (long half-life isotopes)

Bismuth is used in low-melting alloys for fire detectors, extinguishers, and electrical fuses. Its compounds are also used in medical applications for treating stomach and intestinal issues. Due to its low toxicity, bismuth serves as an eco-friendly replacement for lead in many industrial uses.

Historical Perspective

Bismuth has been known since ancient times and was often confused with lead or tin. Its name is thought to have originated from the German word "Wismut," meaning "white mass." Early applications of bismuth included alloys for printing presses and fire safety devices.

In the modern era, bismuth’s unique properties have found uses in diverse fields, from pharmaceuticals to eco-friendly industrial products, marking it as a versatile and fascinating element.
