Lanthanum (La)

Atomic Number 57 | Soft, Malleable Rare Earth Metal

Overview

Lanthanum is a chemical element with the symbol La and atomic number 57. It is a soft, silvery-white metal and the first element in the lanthanide series. Lanthanum is used in various applications, including optical glasses, catalysts, and rechargeable batteries.

Discovered in 1839 by Carl Gustav Mosander, lanthanum is named after the Greek word "lanthanein," meaning "to lie hidden," as it was initially difficult to isolate from cerium.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 57
  • Atomic Mass: 138.91 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Xe] 5d1 6s2
  • Group / Block: f-block (lanthanide series)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 920 °C
  • Boiling Point: 3464 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Lanthanum is widely used in the production of high-quality optical glasses and camera lenses. It is also employed as a catalyst in petroleum refining and in the manufacturing of rechargeable nickel-metal hydride batteries. Lanthanum compounds are used in carbon lighting applications, such as studio lighting and cinema projectors.

Historical Perspective

Lanthanum was first isolated in 1839 by Carl Gustav Mosander while working with cerium nitrate. Its discovery marked the beginning of the identification of rare earth elements. Lanthanum's name reflects its hidden nature, as it was initially challenging to separate from cerium.

Today, lanthanum is a vital material in advanced optics, battery technologies, and catalytic converters, demonstrating its significance in modern industry.
