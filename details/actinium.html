Actinium (Ac)

Atomic Number 89 | Radioactive and Luminescent Metal

Overview

Actinium is a chemical element with the symbol Ac and atomic number 89. It is a soft, silvery-white, radioactive metal that glows faintly in the dark due to its intense radioactivity. Actinium was discovered in 1899 by Friedrich Oskar Giesel and is the first element in the actinide series.

Actinium is extremely rare and is found in trace amounts in uranium and thorium ores. Its primary use is as a neutron source and in radiation therapy for cancer treatment.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 89
  • Atomic Mass: 227 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Rn] 6d1 7s2
  • Group / Block: Actinides (f-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 1050 °C
  • Boiling Point: 3200 °C
  • Radioactive: Yes

Actinium’s strong radioactivity makes it useful as a neutron source and in radiation therapy for cancer. However, its scarcity and intense radioactivity limit its applications to specialized fields.

Actinium in Action

Historical Perspective

Actinium was independently discovered in 1899 by Friedrich Oskar Giesel and named after the Greek word "aktinos," meaning ray or beam, to reflect its radioactivity. It was later confirmed as an element by André-Louis Debierne.

The discovery of actinium marked a significant milestone in understanding the actinide series and the properties of radioactive elements. Today, actinium remains a focus of scientific research and is used in niche medical applications.
