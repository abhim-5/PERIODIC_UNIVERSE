Barium (Ba)

Atomic Number 56 | Alkaline Earth Metal

Overview

Barium is a chemical element with the symbol Ba and atomic number 56. It is a soft, silvery metal that is highly reactive with water and air. Barium is primarily used in industrial applications, including the production of drilling fluids, glassmaking, and radiology.

Discovered in 1808 by Sir Humphry Davy, barium's name is derived from the Greek word "barys," meaning heavy, due to its high density compared to other alkaline earth metals.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 56
  • Atomic Mass: 137.33 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Xe] 6s2
  • Group / Block: 2 (s-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 727 °C
  • Boiling Point: 1845 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Barium is commonly used in drilling fluids for oil and gas exploration, as it increases the density of the fluids. It is also a key ingredient in barium-based contrast agents for medical imaging, particularly in X-ray diagnostics of the gastrointestinal tract. Additionally, barium compounds are used in fireworks to produce green colors and in ceramics and glassmaking.

Historical Perspective

Barium was first isolated in 1808 by Sir Humphry Davy using electrolysis. Its compounds, such as barium sulfate, were used historically for medical and industrial purposes. Over time, barium has found applications in a wide range of fields, from diagnostics to pyrotechnics.

Today, barium continues to be an important material in both industrial and medical applications, demonstrating its versatility and utility.
