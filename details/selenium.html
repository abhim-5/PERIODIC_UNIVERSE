Selenium (Se)

Atomic Number 34 | Photoconductive Nonmetal

Overview

Selenium is a chemical element with the symbol Se and atomic number 34. It is a nonmetal with properties similar to sulfur and tellurium. Selenium is photoconductive, meaning its electrical conductivity increases when exposed to light, making it valuable in photocells, solar panels, and photocopiers.

Discovered in 1817 by Jöns Jacob Berzelius, selenium is named after "Selene," the Greek goddess of the moon. It is found in trace amounts in the Earth's crust and has biological significance as a micronutrient.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 34
  • Atomic Mass: 78.96 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Ar] 3d10 4s2 4p4
  • Group / Block: 16 (p-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 221 °C
  • Boiling Point: 685 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Selenium is widely used in the glass industry to decolorize glass and to produce red-colored glasses and enamels. It is also a critical component in photocells and solar panels. In biology, selenium is a vital trace element required for the proper functioning of certain enzymes and plays a role in antioxidant activity.

Historical Perspective

Selenium was discovered in 1817 by Swedish chemist Jöns Jacob Berzelius while working with sulfuric acid residues. Its name, inspired by the moon, reflects its chemical similarity to tellurium (named after the Earth). Selenium's unique photoconductive properties have made it indispensable in modern electronics and renewable energy technologies.

Today, selenium is a vital material in various industries and an essential micronutrient for human and animal health.
