Francium (Fr)

Atomic Number 87 | Extremely Rare and Radioactive Alkali Metal

Overview

Francium is a chemical element with the symbol Fr and atomic number 87. It is an alkali metal, the heaviest of its group, and extremely radioactive. Francium occurs as a result of the decay of actinium and is incredibly rare in nature, with only trace amounts found on Earth.

Discovered in 1939 by Marguerite Perey, francium was named after France. Due to its rarity and short half-life, it has no significant practical applications and remains largely a subject of scientific research.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 87
  • Atomic Mass: 223 u (most stable isotope)
  • Electron Configuration: [Rn] 7s1
  • Group / Block: 1 (s-block)
  • Standard State: Solid (assumed) at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 27 °C (estimated)
  • Boiling Point: 677 °C (estimated)
  • Radioactive: Yes

Francium has no significant industrial or commercial uses due to its extreme rarity and radioactivity. Its short half-life limits its availability for research purposes. Most studies of francium involve its properties as an alkali metal and its behavior in nuclear reactions.

Historical Perspective

Francium was discovered in 1939 by Marguerite Perey at the Curie Institute in Paris. It was the last element to be discovered in nature rather than synthesized. Perey initially named it "actinium K" before deciding on the name francium to honor her homeland.

Its rarity and highly unstable nature have made francium a challenging element to study. Research on francium continues to focus on its nuclear properties and its place in the periodic table as the heaviest alkali metal.
