Neon (Ne)

Atomic Number 10 | Bright Noble Gas

Overview

Neon is a chemical element with the symbol Ne and atomic number 10. It is a noble gas, colorless under standard conditions, but glows a brilliant reddish-orange when placed in an electric field, as in neon lamps. Despite being relatively rare in the Earth's atmosphere (about 18 parts per million by volume), neon is one of the most abundant elements in the universe.

Neon was discovered in 1898 by Sir William Ramsay and Morris Travers in the residue left after evaporating liquid air. Known for its distinctive glow in advertising signs, neon also plays a role in cryogenic refrigeration.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 10
  • Atomic Mass: 20.180 u
  • Electron Configuration: [He] 2s2 2p6
  • Group / Block: 18 (p-block), Noble Gas
  • Standard State: Gas at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: −248.59 °C
  • Boiling Point: −246.08 °C
  • Radioactive: No (stable isotopes exist)

Neon is used in the iconic neon lights which produce different colors when mixed with other gases. It also has applications in high-voltage indicators, vacuum tubes, and helium-neon lasers. In cryogenics, neon can be a more efficient refrigerant than helium under certain conditions.

Historical Perspective

Neon was the second noble gas discovered by Ramsay and Travers, coming shortly after their discovery of krypton. The name "neon" is derived from the Greek word "neos," meaning "new." In 1910, Georges Claude developed the neon lamp, revealing neon’s signature red glow, which quickly became popular for advertising signs.

Today, neon’s relative rarity on Earth means it commands a higher price compared to other more abundant industrial gases. Nevertheless, its captivating glow remains strongly associated with the early 20th century's golden era of neon signs in major cities like Paris and Las Vegas.
