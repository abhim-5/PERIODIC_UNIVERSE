Bohrium (Bh)

Atomic Number 107 | Synthetic and Highly Radioactive Element

Overview

Bohrium is a chemical element with the symbol Bh and atomic number 107. It is a synthetic element and does not occur naturally. Bohrium is produced in particle accelerators by bombarding lighter elements with heavy ions. Its most stable isotope, Bh-270, has a half-life of about 61 seconds.

Bohrium was first synthesized in 1976 by a Soviet research team at the Joint Institute for Nuclear Research in Dubna and later confirmed by a German research team at the GSI Helmholtz Centre for Heavy Ion Research. The element is named after the Danish physicist Niels Bohr.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 107
  • Atomic Mass: [270] u (most stable isotope)
  • Electron Configuration: [Rn] 5f14 6d5 7s2
  • Group / Block: 7 (d-block)
  • Standard State: Solid (predicted)
  • Melting Point: Unknown
  • Boiling Point: Unknown
  • Radioactive: Yes

Due to its short half-life and instability, bohrium has no practical applications. It is used exclusively in scientific research to study the properties of superheavy elements and to expand our understanding of the periodic table.

Historical Perspective

Bohrium was first synthesized in 1976 by a Soviet team led by Yuri Oganessian. Its discovery was later confirmed by German researchers, and the name "bohrium" was officially adopted in honor of Niels Bohr for his contributions to atomic structure and quantum theory.

As a synthetic element, bohrium continues to be studied to understand its nuclear and chemical properties, as well as its place among the superheavy elements.
