Sodium (Na)

Atomic Number 11 | Essential Alkali Metal

Overview

Sodium is a chemical element with the symbol Na (from the Latin "natrium") and atomic number 11. It is a soft, silvery-white, highly reactive metal and is a member of the alkali metal group in the periodic table. Sodium is essential for life, playing a critical role in nerve function and fluid balance in living organisms.

Discovered in 1807 by Sir Humphry Davy through the electrolysis of molten sodium hydroxide, sodium is abundant in nature, primarily in the form of sodium chloride (common salt).

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 11
  • Atomic Mass: 22.989 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Ne] 3s1
  • Group / Block: 1 (s-block), Alkali Metal
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 97.79 °C
  • Boiling Point: 883 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Sodium is widely used in industries, including the production of chemicals, metal refining, and glass manufacturing. It also plays a key role in water softening and the preservation of food. Sodium vapor lamps are commonly used in street lighting due to their efficiency.

Historical Perspective

Sodium was first isolated by Sir Humphry Davy in 1807 through the electrolysis of sodium hydroxide. Its name comes from "soda," a common term for sodium compounds in the early 19th century. Sodium’s reactivity and abundance in nature have made it a cornerstone of modern industry and technology.

Historically, sodium compounds like sodium carbonate and sodium chloride have been used for thousands of years in glassmaking, food preservation, and soap production, underscoring the element’s essential role in daily life.
