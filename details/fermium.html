Fermium (Fm)

Atomic Number 100 | A Rare Synthetic Radioactive Element

Overview

Fermium is a chemical element with the symbol Fm and atomic number 100. It is a synthetic, radioactive metal in the actinide series. Fermium was first identified in the debris of the first hydrogen bomb test, Ivy Mike, in 1952 by Albert Ghiorso and his team.

Named after Enrico Fermi, a pioneer in nuclear physics, fermium has no significant applications outside of scientific research.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 100
  • Atomic Mass: 257 u (most stable isotope)
  • Electron Configuration: [Rn] 5f12 7s2
  • Group / Block: Actinides (f-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 1527 °C
  • Boiling Point: Unknown
  • Density: 9.7 g/cm³ (estimated)

Fermium is used primarily in scientific research to study the properties of heavy elements. Its high radioactivity and rarity limit its practical applications.

Fermium in Action

Historical Perspective

Fermium was discovered in 1952 by Albert Ghiorso and his team while analyzing the debris from the first hydrogen bomb test. Its name honors Enrico Fermi, a key figure in the development of nuclear physics and quantum theory.

Fermium remains a subject of scientific interest, contributing to our understanding of the periodic table's heaviest elements.
