Niobium (Nb)

Atomic Number 41 | Superconducting Transition Metal

Overview

Niobium is a chemical element with the symbol Nb and atomic number 41. It is a lustrous, gray metal known for its high melting point, corrosion resistance, and superconducting properties. Niobium is commonly used in steel alloys to enhance strength and in superconducting magnets for medical imaging.

Discovered in 1801 by Charles Hatchett, niobium was initially confused with tantalum due to their similar properties. It was later recognized as a distinct element and is now widely used in advanced technologies.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 41
  • Atomic Mass: 92.906 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Kr] 4d4 5s1
  • Group / Block: 5 (d-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 2477 °C
  • Boiling Point: 4744 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Niobium is extensively used in the aerospace and energy industries due to its ability to strengthen steel alloys. It is also a key material in the production of superconducting magnets, used in MRI machines and particle accelerators. Niobium is further utilized in jewelry and coinage because of its hypoallergenic properties and unique colorability.

Niobium in Action

Historical Perspective

Niobium was first discovered in 1801 by Charles Hatchett, who named it columbium in honor of the New World. It was later renamed niobium after Niobe, a figure in Greek mythology, to align with its counterpart, tantalum. Its properties make it a critical material in modern engineering and physics.

Today, niobium is essential in high-tech applications, including superconductors, aerospace components, and renewable energy systems, highlighting its versatility and importance.
