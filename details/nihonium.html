Nihonium (Nh)

Atomic Number 113 | Synthetic and Highly Radioactive Element

Overview

Nihonium is a chemical element with the symbol Nh and atomic number 113. It is a synthetic element that is not found naturally and is produced in particle accelerators. Nihonium is highly unstable, with its most stable isotope, Nh-286, having a half-life of about 20 seconds.

Nihonium was first synthesized in 2004 by a team of Japanese scientists at the RIKEN Nishina Center for Accelerator-Based Science. The element is named after "Nihon," one of the two Japanese words for "Japan."

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 113
  • Atomic Mass: [286] u (most stable isotope)
  • Electron Configuration: [Rn] 5f14 6d10 7s2 7p1
  • Group / Block: 13 (p-block)
  • Standard State: Solid (predicted)
  • Melting Point: Unknown
  • Boiling Point: Unknown
  • Radioactive: Yes

Due to its short half-life and instability, nihonium has no practical applications. It is used exclusively in scientific research to study the properties of superheavy elements and to expand our understanding of the periodic table.

Historical Perspective

Nihonium was first synthesized in 2004 by a team led by Kosuke Morita. Its name pays tribute to Japan, the country where the discovery was made, marking a significant milestone in the field of nuclear chemistry.

Although it has no practical applications, the discovery of nihonium contributes to our understanding of superheavy elements and their behavior.
