Chromium (Cr)

Atomic Number 24 | Shiny Transition Metal

Overview

Chromium is a chemical element with the symbol Cr and atomic number 24. Known for its lustrous and shiny appearance, chromium is a hard and brittle metal often used in electroplating to give other metals a polished finish. It is also a vital component of stainless steel.

Discovered in 1797 by Louis Nicolas Vauquelin, chromium compounds are responsible for the vibrant colors in rubies and emeralds, making it a crucial element in both industry and gemology.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 24
  • Atomic Mass: 51.9961 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Ar] 3d5 4s1
  • Group / Block: 6 (d-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 1907 °C
  • Boiling Point: 2671 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Chromium is widely used in stainless steel production, providing corrosion resistance and hardness. It is also utilized in chrome plating, pigments, and dyes, and plays a role in leather tanning. Chromium compounds are essential in the production of colorful glass and ceramics.

Historical Perspective

Chromium was first isolated in 1797 by Louis Nicolas Vauquelin, who discovered it in the mineral crocoite. Its name comes from the Greek word "chroma," meaning color, due to the brilliant colors of its compounds.

Over time, chromium has become a critical material in metallurgy and decorative applications, ensuring its place in industries ranging from automotive to jewelry.
