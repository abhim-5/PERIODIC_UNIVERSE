Thorium (Th)

Atomic Number 90 | A Naturally Occurring Radioactive Element

Overview

Thorium is a chemical element with the symbol Th and atomic number 90. It is a weakly radioactive, silvery metal that tarnishes in air, forming thorium dioxide. Thorium is abundant in the Earth’s crust and holds potential as a safer alternative to uranium in nuclear energy.

Discovered in 1828 by Swedish chemist Jöns Jacob Berzelius, thorium was named after Thor, the Norse god of thunder. Its use in gas mantles was once widespread, but today it is mainly studied for its potential in next-generation nuclear reactors.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 90
  • Atomic Mass: 232.04 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Rn] 6d2 7s2
  • Group / Block: Actinides (f-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 1750 °C
  • Boiling Point: 4788 °C
  • Density: 11.72 g/cm³

Thorium is used in high-temperature ceramics and specialized glass. Its primary interest lies in its potential use as a nuclear fuel, particularly in thorium reactors, which could provide a cleaner and safer form of nuclear energy.

Historical Perspective

Thorium was discovered in 1828 by Jöns Jacob Berzelius while analyzing a mineral sample. Its name, derived from the Norse god Thor, reflects its strength and potential power. Once widely used in gas mantles, thorium's importance has shifted toward research in nuclear energy.

Today, thorium’s potential as a nuclear fuel is being explored as a safer and more sustainable alternative to uranium, promising advancements in clean energy technology.
