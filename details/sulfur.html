Sulfur (S)

Atomic Number 16 | Element of Life and Industry

Overview

Sulfur is a chemical element with the symbol S and atomic number 16. It is a bright yellow, nonmetallic element that occurs in nature both in its elemental form and as sulfide and sulfate minerals. Sulfur is essential to all living organisms as a component of amino acids and vitamins.

Known since ancient times, sulfur was referred to as "brimstone" in historical texts. It has a wide range of applications, from agriculture to industrial processes, and is crucial for the production of sulfuric acid, one of the most important industrial chemicals.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 16
  • Atomic Mass: 32.06 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Ne] 3s2 3p4
  • Group / Block: 16 (p-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 115.21 °C
  • Boiling Point: 444.6 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Sulfur is widely used in the production of sulfuric acid, which is a key chemical in industries such as fertilizers, petrochemicals, and detergents. It is also used in vulcanization of rubber, gunpowder, and as a fungicide in agriculture. Additionally, sulfur compounds play an important role in biological systems.

Historical Perspective

Sulfur has been known since ancient times and was historically associated with fire and brimstone. In the 18th century, Antoine Lavoisier identified it as an element. Its name is derived from the Latin word "sulphurium."

Sulfur’s importance grew with the Industrial Revolution, particularly with its role in producing sulfuric acid. Today, it remains vital to both industrial applications and biological systems.
