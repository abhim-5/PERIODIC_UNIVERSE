Nobelium (No)

Atomic Number 102 | A Synthetic and Highly Radioactive Element

Overview

Nobelium is a chemical element with the symbol No and atomic number 102. It is a synthetic, radioactive metal in the actinide series. Nobelium was first discovered in 1958 by a team of scientists at the Joint Institute for Nuclear Research in Dubna, Russia, and the University of California, Berkeley.

Named in honor of Alfred Nobel, the inventor of dynamite and founder of the Nobel Prize, nobelium is used exclusively for scientific research.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 102
  • Atomic Mass: 259 u (most stable isotope)
  • Electron Configuration: [Rn] 5f14 7s2
  • Group / Block: Actinides (f-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C (predicted)
  • Melting Point: 827 °C (predicted)
  • Boiling Point: Unknown
  • Density: Unknown

Nobelium is primarily used in scientific research to study the properties of heavy elements and their isotopes. Its high radioactivity and scarcity limit its practical applications.

Nobelium in Action

Historical Perspective

Nobelium was first synthesized in 1958 by scientists in Dubna, Russia, and Berkeley, California. Its name honors Alfred Nobel, reflecting its significance in scientific exploration.

Today, nobelium continues to be a subject of study in the field of nuclear chemistry and physics, aiding in the understanding of the properties of actinides and transuranium elements.
