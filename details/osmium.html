Osmium (Os)

Atomic Number 76 | Densest Naturally Occurring Element

Overview

Osmium is a chemical element with the symbol Os and atomic number 76. It is a hard, brittle, bluish-white transition metal that is the densest naturally occurring element. Osmium is primarily used in alloys and as a catalyst in chemical reactions.

Discovered in 1803 by Smithson Tennant, osmium is named after the Greek word "osme," meaning smell, due to its strong odor when oxidized.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 76
  • Atomic Mass: 190.23 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f14 5d6 6s2
  • Group / Block: 8 (d-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 3033 °C
  • Boiling Point: 5012 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Osmium is often alloyed with other platinum-group metals for applications requiring hardness and durability, such as fountain pen nibs and electrical contacts. It is also used as a catalyst in the chemical industry and in the production of high-precision instruments.

Historical Perspective

Osmium was discovered in 1803 by Smithson Tennant while analyzing residues left after dissolving platinum ores. Its high density and hardness have made it invaluable in industrial and scientific applications.

Today, osmium is an essential material in fields requiring extreme durability and precision, including aerospace, electronics, and chemical engineering.
