Carbon (C)

Atomic Number 6 | Basis of All Known Life

Overview

Carbon is the chemical element with symbol C and atomic number 6. It is nonmetallic and tetravalent—making four electrons available to form covalent chemical bonds. Carbon is the 15th most abundant element in the Earth's crust and the 4th most abundant element in the universe by mass after hydrogen, helium, and oxygen.

Carbon’s unique ability to form diverse bonds creates the backbone of all organic compounds, hence making it essential for life on Earth. Common allotropes include graphite, diamond, and amorphous carbon.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 6
  • Atomic Mass: 12.011 u
  • Electron Configuration: [He] 2s2 2p2
  • Group / Block: 14 (p-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point (Graphite): ~ 3600 °C (sublimes)
  • Boiling Point: ~ 4200 °C (sublimes)
  • Radioactive: No (stable isotopes exist), but C-14 is radioactive, used in radiocarbon dating

Carbon's allotropes range from the hardest natural mineral (diamond) to one of the softest (graphite). Industrially, carbon is crucial in steel production, plastics (organic polymers), and countless other applications.

Historical Perspective

The recognition of carbon as an element stretches back to ancient times, with charcoal being used for millennia. Graphite was once misidentified as a form of lead, giving rise to the term “pencil lead.”

In the late 18th century, Antoine Lavoisier’s experiments helped establish carbon’s elemental nature. Later studies uncovered diamonds’ composition (pure carbon), and modern chemistry has revealed fascinating carbon structures such as fullerenes and graphene.
