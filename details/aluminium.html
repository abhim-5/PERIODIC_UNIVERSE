Aluminium (Al)

Atomic Number 13 | Lightweight Metal

Overview

Aluminium is a chemical element with the symbol Al and atomic number 13. It is a silvery-white, soft, nonmagnetic, and ductile metal in the boron group. Aluminium is the most abundant metal in the Earth's crust and the third most abundant element overall, constituting about 8% of the Earth's solid surface.

Discovered in 1825 by Hans Christian Ørsted, aluminium has become one of the most important engineering materials, prized for its lightweight, corrosion resistance, and recyclability.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 13
  • Atomic Mass: 26.981 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Ne] 3s2 3p1
  • Group / Block: 13 (p-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 660.32 °C
  • Boiling Point: 2519 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Aluminium is used extensively in the aerospace, transportation, and construction industries due to its strength-to-weight ratio. It is a key material in packaging, such as cans and foils, and is widely used in electrical transmission lines. Aluminium compounds are also used in water purification and as catalysts in chemical reactions.

Historical Perspective

Aluminium was first isolated in 1825 by Hans Christian Ørsted. The development of the Hall-Héroult process in 1886 made aluminium production economical and revolutionized its availability and use.

In ancient times, aluminium compounds were used as dyes and in medicine. Today, aluminium is integral to modern technology and infrastructure, representing one of the most versatile and widely used elements.
